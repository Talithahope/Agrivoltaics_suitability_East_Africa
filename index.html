Morogoro, Tanzania

Sustainable Agriculture Tanzania (SAT)

36.6 kW off-grid system with battery storage

This off-grid agrivoltaic system replaced diesel generators at SAT's farmer training centre, generating 12.55 MWh of electricity over the study year — equivalent to an estimated saving of $5,310 per year against Tanzanian grid electricity costs.

Eight crops were tested across three growing seasons. Beans (+123.7%) and Swiss chard (+90.3%) had significantly higher yields under the agrivoltaic system. Maize (+11.8%), kale (−17.2%) and onions in the 2023 season (−15.9%) showed no statistically significant difference. Onions in the 2022 season (−20.7%), sweet pepper (−31.5%) and eggplant (−43.9%) had significantly lower yields — though even the lower-yielding crops still generated the highest incomes per hectare due to their high market value.

Bean survival was substantially higher under the panels: 39% mortality compared with 62% in the control plot during a warm growing period. Maize showed a similar pattern, with 26% mortality under agrivoltaics versus 35% in the control. Panel shading reduced total irrigation use by 12.6%, and when combined with rainwater harvested from panel runoff, overall demand on SAT's centralised irrigation system fell by 14.4%.

Isinya, Kenya

Latia Agribusiness Solutions (LAS)

62.1 kW grid-tied system

This grid-tied agrivoltaic system at Latia's agribusiness headquarters generated 30.13 MWh over the study year, equivalent to 56% of the farm's annual electricity consumption and saving an estimated $5,725 per year against grid costs. If fully utilised, the system could generate savings exceeding $18,000 per year.

Four crops were tested. Kale performed significantly better under agrivoltaics, with heavier and wider leaves and taller plants. Coriander plants directly under a panel produced a significantly higher yield than the control, while those under a gap between panels showed no significant difference. Cabbage and onion both had significantly lower yields under the panels, with smaller and lighter produce — though cabbages grown under a gap between panels were not significantly different from the control.

As at the Tanzania site, combining crop production with electricity generation enhanced land productivity across all growing seasons. A rainwater harvesting system channelling panel runoff into storage tanks supplemented the existing irrigation supply.

What is this map?

This interactive map shows the results of a multi-criteria spatial analysis assessing where agrivoltaic systems — the co-location of solar panels and agriculture — are biophysically suitable across East Africa.

Scenarios

Climate Vulnerable prioritises areas where farming communities face the greatest climate risk, weighting factors like drought frequency, heat stress, and livelihood vulnerability more heavily.

Maximum Productivity prioritises areas where agrivoltaic systems would generate the most combined energy and agricultural output, weighting solar irradiance and crop productivity more heavily.

Difference (CV − MP) highlights where the two scenarios diverge. Positive values (purple) mean an area scores higher under the climate-vulnerable weighting; negative values (orange) mean it scores higher under the maximum-productivity weighting.

Suitability scores

Scores range from 4 (low suitability) to 9 (high suitability). Areas scoring 0 were classified as unsuitable, and areas left transparent were excluded from the analysis entirely (e.g. water bodies, protected areas, steep terrain).

Trial sites

The orange triangles mark real-world agrivoltaic trial sites in Kenya (Isinya) and Tanzania (Morogoro) that informed and validated the analysis. Click them to see field trial results from Randle-Boggis et al. (2025).
